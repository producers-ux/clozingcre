The Model

District Vision just proved the format. We run it further.

District Vision opened its first-ever permanent flagship in LA's Arts District in May 2026 — and built the entire concept around meditation as much as retail. They call it a "hybrid retail and research environment." Programming runs after hours: breathwork, running, meditation in the same room where product lives during the day.

The format is proven. We're borrowing the logic and running it without the retail layer — which makes the concept cleaner and the building owner pitch simpler.

The Space

The ceiling was
always the point.

850 N Palm Canyon Drive — the former Arthur Elrod studio — has a lighted ceiling built into the architecture of the building. It is not a temporary installation. It is not a projection system. It is a feature of the space that most uses have treated as background.

A lie-down meditation session makes the ceiling the entire experience. Participants recline, look up, and the room becomes an interior sky — controlled, still, and completely specific to this address.

The activation doesn't import an experience into the building. It surfaces what the building already is. That is the argument to the building owner: the asset is already there.

Architectural Feature · Built-In · Not Temporary

The lighted ceiling at 850

The ceiling light system at 850 is part of the building's existing infrastructure. In standard tenancy, it functions as ambient illumination. In a lie-down meditation context, it becomes the primary visual element of the session.

Sessions use the ceiling as-is. No rigging. No permitting. No build-out. The architecture does the work.

The Format

Not seated.
Not standing.
Horizontal.

The lie-down format is the differentiator. Most urban meditation programming is seated — cushions, chairs, benches facing a wall or a facilitator. District Vision is no exception.

Lying down changes the relationship to the room entirely. The ceiling becomes the horizon. Peripheral vision softens. The body releases the effort of sitting. Sessions land deeper and require less from participants — which matters for a walk-up audience that didn't come planning to meditate.

"The body releases the postural effort of sitting. Sessions land deeper and require less."

For Palm Springs specifically: this maps directly onto what already works here. Sound baths are already horizontal. Desert culture already understands stillness and looking up — at mountains, at stars, at open sky. We bring that behavior indoors, under a ceiling built to hold it.

The Artist

Jenna Marie Hansen.
Work in the building.

Jenna Marie Hansen is a figurative artist whose practice centers on continuous line work — gesture as language, the body as a carrier of emotion and connection. Her work holds a room rather than fills it.

Her presence in 850 gives the activation something District Vision cannot buy: an artist whose work belongs to this space. The building owner isn't hosting a pop-up. They are hosting an artist's environment.

Sessions are held in the presence of her work. The ceiling is above. The figures are present. Participants lie in the middle of both — held between architecture and art.

"The spirit uses energetics. She materializes what is invisible."

The combination is specific to this address. It cannot be replicated elsewhere on the corridor. That specificity is the pitch to the building owner and the press hook for the activation.

Programming

Three formats.
One ceiling.

All sessions are lie-down. All sessions are 30–45 minutes. Capacity is kept small by design — 12 to 20 participants — intimate enough to feel personal, repeatable enough to run multiple times daily.

01

Morning Meditation

Guided and silent. Participants recline and look up. A facilitator leads a short opening then holds space for stillness under the ceiling. Minimal or no music.

30 min · 8–12 participants · 7–9 AM
02

Sound Bath

Crystal bowls, gongs, or live instrument played in the room. Sound moves through the space; the ceiling holds the visual field. The most accessible format for walk-up visitors.

45 min · 12–20 participants · Midday
03

Breathwork

Guided breathwork with a local facilitator. Participants remain reclined throughout. Evening sessions targeting Palm Springs residents as an after-work offering.

40 min · 8–14 participants · Late Afternoon
For the Building Owner

What this does for 850.

Vacant space costs more than the absence of rent. It signals stagnation on a corridor that prospective tenants are watching. This activation keeps 850 alive and in motion while long-term tenant outreach runs in parallel — the space is occupied, programmed, and generating attention rather than sitting dark.

The June–September window is deliberate. Print publications — Palm Springs Life, Desert Magazine, regional lifestyle press — work on lead times of two to four months. An activation that opens in June can land in fall editorial. A vacant space cannot.

  • Keeps the space activated during tenant outreach. Programming runs while we work the market. Every session is a reason for someone to walk through the door — including prospective tenants, their brokers, and the people who talk to both.
  • Print press lead times require a long window. Regional and national wellness publications plan two to four months out. June–September gives us enough runway to pitch fall issues of Palm Springs Life, Desert Magazine, and wellness verticals. A shorter pop-up gets social coverage. This window gets ink.
  • Gives prospective tenants a reason to visit. A floor plan is an abstraction. A functioning, attended space is evidence. Prospects who experience the room during the activation understand what it can hold — and arrive with an idea already forming.
  • No build-out. Mats, sound equipment, soft goods. Everything comes out when the pilot ends. No tenant improvement dollars at risk.
  • Foot traffic with data. Ticketed sessions generate attendance records and email capture — a documented audience that belongs to the address.
  • O+R relationship. 850 becomes the named pilot for the Range series — an ongoing curatorial relationship, not a one-time rental.
